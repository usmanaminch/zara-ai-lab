Seeing Every Skin: AI in Dermatology and the Health Equity Gap

Zara  ·  June 2026  ·  Independent research

Imagine visiting a doctor and being told your diagnosis is less accurate simply because of the color of your skin. For millions of patients with darker skin tones around the world, this is not hypothetical — it is an emerging reality in the age of artificial intelligence. As AI tools become increasingly embedded in medical practice, the field of dermatology stands at a critical crossroads: these systems hold enormous promise, yet they risk encoding and amplifying the very inequalities medicine has long struggled to address.

The Promise of AI in Dermatology

Dermatology has become one of medicine's most data-rich fields, making it a natural fit for AI. Deep learning algorithms trained on thousands of skin images can now identify conditions — from melanoma to eczema — with accuracy that rivals experienced dermatologists. A systematic review published in 2025 found that AI-based dermatology systems achieve a pooled diagnostic accuracy of 0.88 on the AUROC scale, comparable to specialist-level performance.

This matters especially because dermatology is one of the most inaccessible specialties in medicine. There is a national shortage of dermatologists, with providers concentrated in urban centers while rural and underserved communities wait months for an appointment. Studies show that Black and Hispanic patients are significantly less likely to receive outpatient dermatology care than white patients, and per capita spending on dermatology visits for Black patients is roughly one-third that of white patients. AI diagnostic tools, accessible through smartphones and primary care clinics, could theoretically extend specialist-level care to anyone with a camera and a skin concern.

"AI diagnostic tools could extend specialist-level care to anyone with a camera and a skin concern — but only if they work equally well for everyone."

The Problem Hidden in the Data

The challenge lies in how these AI systems are built. Every AI model learns from training data — in this case, images of skin conditions. And the most widely used dermatology datasets, including the International Skin Imaging Collaboration (ISIC) dataset, are heavily skewed toward lighter skin tones, with Fitzpatrick skin types V and VI — the darkest tones — dramatically underrepresented. In some benchmarks, lighter skin tones account for over 70 percent of images. When a model learns primarily from lighter skin, it learns to recognize disease as it appears on lighter skin. The result is predictable: AI systems consistently underperform on patients with darker skin tones.

The numbers are striking. A 2025 meta-analysis found that AI diagnostic performance dropped from an AUROC of 0.89 for lighter skin tones to 0.82 for darker ones — a gap that could translate into thousands of missed or delayed diagnoses at scale. A study assessing GPT-4's diagnostic accuracy found similar disparities in how AI language models handle skin conditions across skin tones. Research from Northwestern University confirmed that while AI assistance improves overall diagnostic accuracy for physicians, the gap between light and dark skin tone accuracy actually widens when AI is introduced — because doctors tend to trust AI suggestions more readily for patients whose skin resembles the training data.

A Double Burden

What makes this particularly troubling is that patients with darker skin tones already face compounding barriers to dermatology care. Dermatology is one of the least diverse specialties in medicine, and this lack of representation in the workforce means less clinical familiarity with how conditions present across the full range of human skin tones. Fitzpatrick skin types IV through VI — which represent a significant portion of the global population — remain the least studied and most underserved in both clinical training and AI model development. This is a gap that AI, as currently designed, does not close. It deepens it.

The consequences reach beyond inconvenience. Delayed diagnosis of melanoma is associated with significantly worse outcomes and higher mortality. Untreated psoriasis carries serious cardiovascular and mental health risks. When diagnostic tools — whether human or algorithmic — perform worse on specific populations, the downstream effects on health outcomes are real and serious.

What Can Be Done

The good news is that the problem, once identified, has tractable solutions. Research shows that when AI models are trained on more diverse datasets — including images with artificially augmented skin tones — their accuracy on darker skin tones improves meaningfully. Several institutions are now building more representative dermatology databases as a direct response to these findings. Regulatory bodies including the FDA are beginning to explore how skin tone criteria should factor into medical device approval.

But technical fixes alone are insufficient. The deeper issue is representation: in training data, in clinical trials, in medical textbooks, and in the dermatology workforce itself. The American Academy of Dermatology launched a diversity, equity, and inclusion initiative in 2021, recognizing that building a more representative workforce is inseparable from building more equitable care. When dermatologists with diverse backgrounds enter the field, they bring clinical knowledge that improves diagnosis for patients who look like them — and they bring perspectives that shape how AI tools are designed and validated.

A Personal Note

I began thinking about this topic while building AI tools for skincare and skin health education. As I worked on projects like an ingredient analyzer and a skin concern advisor, I kept returning to the same question: who are these tools for? The most enthusiastic users of skincare AI are often young people with a wide range of skin tones — yet the AI systems underlying these tools, and the dermatology datasets they draw from, were built with a much narrower patient in mind.

The promise of AI in medicine is that it can democratize access to expertise. But a tool that works well for some patients and poorly for others is not democratization — it is the automation of existing inequality. Getting this right matters not just for dermatology, but as a test case for how medicine integrates AI responsibly across every specialty.

The technology to close this gap exists. The question is whether the field will prioritize building it for everyone.

Scroll, Buy, Regret? How Social Media is Reshaping Teen Skincare

Zara  ·  June 2026  ·  Independent research

Open TikTok on any given day and within minutes you will encounter a teenager applying retinol, a twelve-year-old reviewing a $60 vitamin C serum, or a "get ready with me" video featuring a ten-step routine lifted from a thirty-five-year-old influencer. Welcome to #DermTok — the corner of the internet where skincare has become a spectator sport, a shopping habit, and for a growing number of young people, a source of genuine confusion and harm.

Social media has done something no dermatology clinic ever could: made skin health a topic of everyday conversation for millions of teenagers. That is, in many ways, a remarkable thing. But it has also created a landscape where misinformation spreads faster than sunscreen, where the line between expert and influencer is almost impossible to see, and where teens are spending hundreds of dollars on products that may be actively hurting their skin. The truth, as with most things, lives somewhere in the middle.

The Good: Democratizing Skin Knowledge

Before social media, teenagers with skin concerns had limited options: see a dermatologist (expensive, often inaccessible), ask a parent (who may not know), or guess. Social media changed that equation. Platforms like TikTok and Instagram gave millions of young people access to information about ingredients, routines, and skin conditions that previously lived only in medical offices or academic journals.

The benefits are real. Research published in 2026 found that people who regularly consumed dermatologist-authored content on social media were more than twice as likely to follow a structured skincare routine compared to those relying on influencer material. For teens in rural communities — where access to dermatologists is severely limited — social media has become a primary healthcare resource. In some cases, it is the only resource. A 2025 study of rural high school students found that 87 percent cited social media as their primary source of skincare information, underscoring how completely these platforms have replaced traditional pathways to dermatologic guidance.

There is also a community dimension that should not be dismissed. For teenagers dealing with acne, eczema, or other visible skin conditions, #DermTok offers something dermatology offices rarely provide: the sense that you are not alone. Comment sections under acne videos are filled with teens sharing experiences, tips, and encouragement in ways that can be genuinely supportive — and that no prescription can replicate.

The Bad: Misinformation at Scale

The problem is that the vast majority of dermatology content on social media is not created by dermatologists. A study published in the Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology found that only 2.5 percent of the most viewed and liked skincare videos on TikTok are created by board-certified dermatologists. On Instagram, the number is similarly dismal: only 4 percent of dermatology-related accounts are managed by medical professionals. The rest — the overwhelming majority — are influencers, enthusiasts, and teenagers sharing what worked for them, with no scientific training and no accountability.

Only 2.5 percent of the most viewed skincare videos on TikTok are created by board-certified dermatologists. The rest are influencers with no medical training and no accountability.

This matters because around 40 percent of people use social media for information on dermatological conditions. When the creators driving that information are not experts, the results can be harmful. Dermatology clinics across the country have reported seeing teenagers presenting with compromised skin barriers, acne flares, and chemical irritation — all caused by products they discovered online and applied without guidance. One of the most striking trends: teenagers and even tweens spending $500 to $1,000 a month on anti-aging products their skin does not need and, in some cases, cannot tolerate.

Specific trends illustrate the problem. Slugging — sealing the face with petroleum jelly overnight — went viral as a hydration hack, but can trap bacteria and worsen acne in oily or acne-prone skin. Skin cycling became a popular routine framework, but the rigid schedule promoted online often ignores individual skin needs. Perhaps most concerning: the widespread promotion of retinol and other potent actives to pre-teens and young teenagers, whose skin is not only still developing but also far more sensitive than the adult skin these products were designed for.

The Ugly: Standards Nobody Can Meet

Beyond the products and the misinformation lies a more insidious problem: what social media is quietly doing to how teenagers feel about their skin. The feeds that teens scroll through are curated, filtered, and often digitally altered. The glass skin trend — poreless, luminous, impossibly smooth — is a beauty standard that does not exist in nature. The tutorials showing before-and-afters often use lighting tricks, filters, and selectively staged photography. The result is an entire generation measuring their real, living skin against images that have been processed to remove every pore, every red spot, every human imperfection.

Research published in 2025 examined the psychological impact of dermatology-related social media on adolescents and found significant links between heavy consumption of skincare content and negative self-perception, particularly around skin appearance. Teenagers who regularly watched skin-focused content reported higher levels of dissatisfaction with their own skin — not because their skin had changed, but because their frame of reference had. The skin they saw in the mirror was being compared not to other real teenagers, but to a digital ideal.

What Would Actually Help

The answer is not to ban teenagers from skincare content — that ship has sailed. Social media is too embedded in how young people learn, connect, and form identity to be wished away. The more productive question is how to make the ecosystem healthier.

Part of the answer lies with platforms. TikTok and Instagram could do significantly more to label content by creator credentials — distinguishing board-certified dermatologists from non-expert creators in the way they already flag news content during elections. A simple verification badge visible on health content would help viewers make informed choices about who to trust.

Part of the answer lies with dermatologists themselves. The minority of dermatologists who are active on social media reach enormous audiences and their presence meaningfully shifts the quality of information available. More dermatologists on these platforms means more signal in a very noisy space.

And part of the answer lies with teenagers themselves. The most protective thing a young person can develop is not a ten-step routine — it is the habit of asking one question before trusting any advice: who is telling me this, and why should I trust them? In a landscape where anyone can become a skincare authority overnight, critical thinking is the best ingredient of all.

A Final Note

I am a teenager who uses social media and cares about skincare. I have seen the get ready with me videos, clicked on the trending products, and felt the pull of the glass-skin ideal. I am also someone who has spent time building AI tools around skin health and reading the research behind dermatological claims. What I have come to believe is this: social media is not the enemy of good skincare. Uncritical consumption of it is.

Used thoughtfully — following verified experts, questioning trends, and understanding that no filter can replicate what honest science can tell you — the internet is an extraordinary resource for skin health. The challenge for this generation is learning to use it that way.
