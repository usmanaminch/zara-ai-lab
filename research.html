Seeing Every Skin: AI in Dermatology and the Health Equity Gap

Zara  ·  June 2026  ·  Independent research

Imagine visiting a doctor and being told your diagnosis is less accurate simply because of the color of your skin. For millions of patients with darker skin tones around the world, this is not hypothetical — it is an emerging reality in the age of artificial intelligence. As AI tools become increasingly embedded in medical practice, the field of dermatology stands at a critical crossroads: these systems hold enormous promise, yet they risk encoding and amplifying the very inequalities medicine has long struggled to address.

The Promise of AI in Dermatology

Dermatology has become one of medicine's most data-rich fields, making it a natural fit for AI. Deep learning algorithms trained on thousands of skin images can now identify conditions — from melanoma to eczema — with accuracy that rivals experienced dermatologists. A systematic review published in 2025 found that AI-based dermatology systems achieve a pooled diagnostic accuracy of 0.88 on the AUROC scale, comparable to specialist-level performance.

This matters especially because dermatology is one of the most inaccessible specialties in medicine. There is a national shortage of dermatologists, with providers concentrated in urban centers while rural and underserved communities wait months for an appointment. AI diagnostic tools, accessible through smartphones and primary care clinics, could theoretically extend specialist-level care to anyone with a camera and a skin concern.

"AI diagnostic tools could extend specialist-level care to anyone with a camera and a skin concern — but only if they work equally well for everyone."

The Problem Hidden in the Data

The challenge lies in how these AI systems are built. Every AI model learns from training data — in this case, images of skin conditions. And the most widely used dermatology datasets are heavily skewed toward lighter skin tones, with Fitzpatrick skin types V and VI dramatically underrepresented. When a model learns primarily from lighter skin, it learns to recognize disease as it appears on lighter skin. The result is predictable: AI systems consistently underperform on patients with darker skin tones.

The numbers are striking. A 2025 meta-analysis found that AI diagnostic performance dropped from an AUROC of 0.89 for lighter skin tones to 0.82 for darker ones — a gap that could translate into thousands of missed or delayed diagnoses at scale.

A Double Burden

What makes this particularly troubling is that patients with darker skin tones already face compounding barriers to dermatology care. Dermatology is one of the least diverse specialties in medicine, and this lack of representation in the workforce means less clinical familiarity with how conditions present across the full range of human skin tones. Fitzpatrick skin types IV through VI — which represent a significant portion of the global population — remain the least studied and most underserved in both clinical training and AI model development.

What Can Be Done

The good news is that the problem, once identified, has tractable solutions. Research shows that when AI models are trained on more diverse datasets, their accuracy on darker skin tones improves meaningfully. Several institutions are now building more representative dermatology databases. Regulatory bodies including the FDA are beginning to explore how skin tone criteria should factor into medical device approval.

But technical fixes alone are insufficient. The deeper issue is representation: in training data, in clinical trials, in medical textbooks, and in the dermatology workforce itself. The technology to close this gap exists. The question is whether the field will prioritize building it for everyone.

Scroll, Buy, Regret? How Social Media is Reshaping Teen Skincare

Zara  ·  June 2026  ·  Independent research

Open TikTok on any given day and within minutes you will encounter a teenager applying retinol, a twelve-year-old reviewing a $60 vitamin C serum, or a "get ready with me" video featuring a ten-step routine lifted from a thirty-five-year-old influencer. Welcome to #DermTok — the corner of the internet where skincare has become a spectator sport, a shopping habit, and for a growing number of young people, a source of genuine confusion and harm.

Social media has done something no dermatology clinic ever could: made skin health a topic of everyday conversation for millions of teenagers. That is, in many ways, a remarkable thing. But it has also created a landscape where misinformation spreads faster than sunscreen, where the line between expert and influencer is almost impossible to see, and where teens are spending hundreds of dollars on products that may be actively hurting their skin. The truth, as with most things, lives somewhere in the middle.

The Good: Democratizing Skin Knowledge

Before social media, teenagers with skin concerns had limited options: see a dermatologist (expensive, often inaccessible), ask a parent (who may not know), or guess. Social media changed that equation. Platforms like TikTok and Instagram gave millions of young people access to information about ingredients, routines, and skin conditions that previously lived only in medical offices or academic journals.

The benefits are real. Research published in 2026 found that people who regularly consumed dermatologist-authored content on social media were more than twice as likely to follow a structured skincare routine. A 2025 study of rural high school students found that 87 percent cited social media as their primary source of skincare information.

"Only 2.5% of the most viewed skincare videos on TikTok are created by board-certified dermatologists. The rest are influencers with no medical training and no accountability."

The Bad: Misinformation at Scale

The problem is that the vast majority of dermatology content on social media is not created by dermatologists. A study published in the Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology found that only 2.5 percent of the most viewed skincare videos on TikTok are created by board-certified dermatologists. Dermatology clinics have reported seeing teenagers with compromised skin barriers, acne flares, and chemical irritation — all caused by products discovered online. One of the most striking trends: teenagers spending $500 to $1,000 a month on anti-aging products their skin does not need.

Specific trends illustrate the problem. Slugging went viral as a hydration hack but can trap bacteria and worsen acne. Skin cycling often ignores individual skin needs. The widespread promotion of retinol to pre-teens, whose skin is far more sensitive, is perhaps most concerning.

The Ugly: Standards Nobody Can Meet

Beyond the products and the misinformation lies a more insidious problem: what social media is quietly doing to how teenagers feel about their skin. The glass skin trend — poreless, luminous, impossibly smooth — is a beauty standard that does not exist in nature. Research published in 2025 found significant links between heavy consumption of skincare content and negative self-perception in adolescents.

What Would Actually Help

The answer is not to ban teenagers from skincare content. Platforms could do more to label content by creator credentials. More dermatologists on these platforms means more signal in a very noisy space. And teenagers themselves can develop the most protective habit: asking who is telling them something, and why they should trust them.

A Final Note

I am a teenager who uses social media and cares about skincare. What I have come to believe is this: social media is not the enemy of good skincare. Uncritical consumption of it is. Used thoughtfully, the internet is an extraordinary resource for skin health. The challenge for this generation is learning to use it that way.

The Invisible Ecosystem: Understanding the Skin Microbiome

Zara  ·  July 2026  ·  Independent research

Here's something that might surprise you: no matter how thoroughly you wash your face, your skin is never truly clean. Right now, trillions of bacteria, fungi, and other tiny organisms are living on its surface. And that is actually a good thing. This community of microscopic life — called the skin microbiome — is one of the most important players in skin health. We are only beginning to understand how much it matters.

Think of your skin as a neighborhood

Your skin is home to somewhere between 1,000 and 1 million bacteria per square centimeter. The type of bacteria depends on where you look. Oily areas like your nose and forehead attract different species than dry areas like your arms. Warm, damp spots have their own communities entirely. It is less like a uniform coating and more like a neighborhood, where different streets have different residents.

Two of the most talked-about residents are Staphylococcus epidermidis — a generally helpful bacteria that blocks harmful invaders — and Cutibacterium acnes, the bacteria linked to acne. Neither is inherently the enemy. It is the balance between them, and between thousands of other species on your skin, that determines whether your skin is healthy or struggling.

When the community is balanced and diverse, scientists call that state eubiosis. Your skin barrier works well, your immune system gets the right signals, and your skin stays hydrated. When that balance tips — when certain species overgrow and crowd out others — it is called dysbiosis. And dysbiosis shows up on your skin in ways most people recognize.

When things get out of balance

A major 2025 review found that dysbiosis plays a direct role in four of the most common skin conditions: acne, eczema, rosacea, and psoriasis. Each works differently, but the pattern is the same — a loss of microbial variety and an overgrowth of one or two problematic species.

Take acne. Most people assume it is caused simply by the presence of Cutibacterium acnes on their skin — but this bacteria lives on almost everyone's face, including people who never break out. What actually matters is the strain and the proportion. When sebum increases — often due to puberty or hormonal changes — the proportion of inflammatory strains can rise, setting off the cycle of clogged pores and breakouts.

Eczema tells a different story. Skin affected by eczema tends to have dramatically less microbial diversity, with Staphylococcus aureus often dominating. A 2024 study found that this imbalance may appear before eczema symptoms develop. Rosacea is linked to microscopic mites called Demodex folliculorum whose populations appear to be partly controlled by the surrounding microbial community.

A 2024 review of 38 studies found that most skin conditions come with decreased microbial diversity — suggesting that a rich, varied microbiome is quietly protective in ways we are only starting to map.

What your skincare is actually doing

Most people pick skincare products based on what they add — hydration, SPF, active ingredients like retinol. Far fewer think about what those products do to the living community already on their skin.

Harsh cleansers — especially those with antibacterial agents like triclosan — do not discriminate between harmful and helpful bacteria. After repeated use, they can leave the skin with a less diverse, less resilient microbial community. The pH of a product also plays a role. Healthy skin sits at a slightly acidic pH of around 4.5 to 5.5. Many cleansers sit at higher pH levels, temporarily disrupting this balance with every wash.

But there is good news. Products containing prebiotics (ingredients that feed good bacteria), probiotics (live beneficial bacteria), and postbiotics (useful byproducts of bacterial activity) are showing real results. A 2026 review of 33 studies found that postbiotic treatments for acne reduced lesion counts by nearly 50% — competing with antibiotic treatments, without the risk of antibiotic resistance.

Where dermatology is headed

The skin microbiome is a young field. Most of the key research has been published in the last five years. But the direction is clear: dermatology is moving away from eliminate what is bad and toward restore what is balanced. Microbiome transplant therapies have already shown early promise for eczema. The idea of skincare tailored to a person's individual microbial profile is no longer science fiction.

Your skin has always been an ecosystem. Dermatology is finally starting to treat it like one.
